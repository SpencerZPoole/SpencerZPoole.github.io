How To Claim Your Own Domain

Every day, over 100,000 .com domain names are registered. From google.com, apple.com, youtube.com, and thousands more; domain names become memorable destinations that are engrained into our everyday lives. For this reason, to the startup businesses or independent entrepreneur, creating a website can be a vital component to success. In the same hand, claiming a domain name that is simple, catchy, and easy to remember is a crucial contributor towards the effectiveness of the site. Once a catchy domain name has been chosen, how is it that a business or individual sets up their website's address and actually hosts their content on the world wide web? How would they even check if their 'catchy' domain name hasn't already been claimed by someone else?

Registrars

The simple truth is that anyone can buy a domain name. All that you have to do is visit a domain name registrar, such as A2, GoDaddy, Namecheap, or Google Domains. At those client-side sites, you can type in and search your custom domain name and check its availability. As long as your domain name suffix (".com", ".org", ".dev", etc.) is valid and another person or business hasn't already registered your domain name, you can pay a fee and buy your domain! Domains typically cost anywhere from one dollar to millions of dollars depending on the demand of your name. CarInsurance.com for example, sold for almost 50 million dollars! It is important to note however, that once you pay a fee, you are actually renting your domain on a (typically) yearly basis, and it is not actually yours to control forever! Most registrars still reserve the right to revoke your rented domain name for legal reasons (for example, if you are using your website for illegal or other 'unacceptable' purposes).

The domain belongs to the registrar - not you.

Once you have payed the registrar the fee for your domain, the domain name registry must be updated with your information. This simply means that there is typically a 'wait period' before your new domain will actually become hosted and available for you to use. While this can take up to 7 days, most registrars typically expect a wait period of around 48 hours.

Alternatives

There are, of course, other alternatives to dealing with a registrar. There are web hosting services such as DreamHost that act not only as registrars but offer to host your website on private servers and feature-rich platforms. There are also many services that offer domain names and server hosting for free (yola, Squarespace, Wix), but the 'free' end of the deal is usually accompanied by a 'watermark' on your domain name of some kind.

As a single specific example; instead of 'catchydomain.com', your 'free' registrar (named, let's say, 'HosterCo') could force the final domain name for your website to be 'catchydomain.hosterco.com'.

On the plus side, however, these free web hosting services often provide features (such as visual programming) that make managing and designing your website much easier for the average novice. These tools have limitations in control of your website's appearance however, and are usually better left alone by the advanced user.

A personal recommendation of mine for a 'free' registrar is GitHub Pages. GitHub Pages allows you to host your website for free from a GitHub repo. If you are already familiar with Git and Github, this will make designing, managing, and interacting with your site much easier via a smooth, familiar, and simple workflow. The only 'catch' of the free end of the deal is that your domain name will be the URL of your repo; ie. 'http://username.github.io/repository'. However, if you'd like to use a custom domain name that you have already registered, GitHub pages supports importing and using it for your "GitHub hosted" website.
